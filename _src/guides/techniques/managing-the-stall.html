How to Manage the Brisket Stall

You've had the brisket on since before sunrise. The temperature climbed nicely all morning, and then around 155°F it just... stopped. An hour goes by. Still 155. You start wondering if your thermometer died, or your fire, or your judgment. Take a breath. This is the stall, it happens to everyone, and nothing is wrong.

What the stall actually is

A brisket is mostly water. As it heats up, that water rises to the surface and evaporates, and evaporation cools things down, the same way sweat cools you off on a hot day. For a few hours, the cooling from all that evaporation almost exactly cancels out the heat your smoker is putting in. The internal temperature flatlines even though you're doing everything right.

That's it. No magic, no broken equipment. It's a wet piece of meat sweating. Once the surface dries out enough, the temperature starts climbing again on its own.

Why it trips people up

The stall lands at the worst possible moment. You're hours in, dinner is on the clock, and the number you've watched all day quits moving. So people start fiddling. They crank the heat, they crack the lid every ten minutes to look, they talk themselves into believing it'll never finish. All of that just drags the cook out longer.

The move during the stall is simple: leave the lid shut and trust your probe. And if the only thing you're going by is the dome thermometer on the lid, you're half blind. You want a probe in the thickest part of the flat. A good instant-read lets you confirm that reading in a second without standing over an open pit, and it's the tool I'd tell anyone to buy first.

Your three options

You can deal with the stall in three ways, and none of them is wrong. It comes down to how much time you have and how much you care about bark.

1. Wait it out

Do nothing. Keep the smoker steady at 225–250°F, leave the lid shut, and let the brisket work through it. This gives you the best bark because the surface keeps drying and setting the whole time. The catch is time: a wait-it-out stall can run three or four hours, so you need to have started early. If you want to know exactly when to put the brisket on to hit dinner, run your weight through the brisket calculator and it'll work backward from your serve time.

2. Wrap it (the Texas crutch)

Wrapping the brisket in foil or butcher paper stops the evaporation, which means the stall basically disappears. The internal temperature starts climbing again almost immediately. Foil is the fastest and also traps steam, which softens the bark you worked to build. Butcher paper is the middle path: it blocks most of the evaporative cooling but still breathes, so the bark survives. Most people wrap once they hit the stall, around 160–170°F.

3. Bump the heat

You can also just run the pit hotter. Going from 225°F to 275°F pushes in more heat than the evaporation can shed, so the stall gets shorter. You trade away a little of the texture you get from a long low cook, but plenty of great brisket comes off a 275°F pit. When the clock is tight and you don't want to wrap, this is the move.

How long it lasts

If you ride it out, expect two to four hours for a full packer at 225°F. A small brisket on a hot pit can move sooner, and a big packer on a cool one can sit there longer. Wrap it and you're looking at an hour or less. Either way, the stall always ends. Plan for the time, keep the lid shut, and let it cook.
