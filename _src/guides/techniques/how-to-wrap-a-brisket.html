How to Wrap a Brisket

Wrapping is one of those choices that feels enormous the first time and obvious by your tenth cook. The short version: foil is fast and soft, paper is the balanced middle, and no wrap gives the best bark if you've got the time. Here's how to pick.

When to wrap

Most people wrap when the brisket hits the stall, somewhere around 160–170°F internal, once the bark has set and turned a deep mahogany. Wrap too early and you never build bark; wait too long and you're just adding time. If you want the full picture on the stall itself, start with managing the stall.

Foil vs paper vs naked

Foil seals completely. It blasts through the stall and braises the meat, which makes it tender fast but steams the bark soft. Butcher paper breathes a little, so it still speeds things up while keeping most of the bark. Going naked, no wrap at all, gives the firmest bark and the deepest smoke, at the cost of the longest cook.

Unwaxed Butcher Paper

What a lot of brisket cooks reach for. Gets you through the stall while keeping the bark firmer than foil does.
